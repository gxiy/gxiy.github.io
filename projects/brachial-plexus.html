--- layout: project-post title: Brachial Plexus Shoulder Model ---

I designed and created a model for brachial plexus palsy as part of Rice University's Summer Experience in Engineering Design Program (SEED).

Background

As part of Rice University's Summer Experience in Engineering Design Program (SEED), I worked with a team of the 4 students to develop an educational model for the Texas Children's Hospital. Our client, a physical therapist, specialized in the treatment of brachial plexus palsy.

Brachial plexus palsy is a condition involving the loss of nerve function in the brachial plexus, the nerves that control the shoulder and arm. It affects primarily newborn infants. Although most infants can recover nerve function, prevention requires physical therapy exercises 5-7 times a day.

Our team was tasked with creating a simple model to allow parents to learn the exercises before performing them on their children.

Description

To develop an educational model for infant brachial plexus palsy therapy, my team followed a design process that included:

  • Identifying the problem
  • Defining design objectives and constraints
  • Brainstorming ideas
  • Building prototypes
  • Testing the final product

We gave our client frequent updates and collaborated with him to develop our prototypes.

Our final product consisted of a model with two shoulder joints. The markings on the two scapulas indicate whether the joint behaves normally (check mark) or behaves abnormally (x mark, sign of palsy). The model could demonstrate four physical therapy exercises and the effect of brachial plexus palsy on the joint when performing the exercises.

The model is primarily made of laser cut wood. The scapulas are attached to the body by compression springs surrounded by heat shrink tubing, and the wooden arms are attached to the body by clasps, torsion springs, and some 3D printed components.
