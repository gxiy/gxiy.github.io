--- layout: project-post title: Brachial Plexus Shoulder Model ---

I designed and created a model for brachial plexus palsy as part of Rice University's Summer Experience in Engineering Design Program (SEED).

Background

Brachial plexus palsy is a condition involving the loss of nerve function in the brachial plexus, the nerves that control the shoulder and arm. It affects primarily newborn infants. Although most infants can recover nerve function, prevention requires physical therapy exercises 5-7 times a day. Our client, a physical therapist at Texas Children's Hospital, wanted a simple model to allow parents to learn the exercises before performing the exercises on their children.

Summary

I worked with a team of 4 students to develop an educational model for infant brachial plexus palsy therapy. My team followed the engineering design process which included:

  • Identifying the problem
  • Defining design objectives and constraints
  • Brainstorming ideas
  • Building prototypes
  • Testing the final product

We gave our client frequent updates and collaborated with him to develop our prototypes.

Our final product consisted of a model with two shoulder joints (one mimicking a joint affected by palsy, the other mimicking a regular joint). The model could demonstrate four physical therapy exercises and the effect of brachial plexus palsy on the joint when performing the exercises.
